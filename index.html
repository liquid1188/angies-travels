Three days in Rome, the slow way.

Skip the bus tour. The city rewards walking, and the trick is knowing which neighborhoods to put on your feet first.

Romans don't rush. The first thing the city teaches you, if you're paying attention, is that there's no point trying to see everything in three days. The trick is to give up on completeness early — like a good Italian — and choose three things to do well instead of thirty things to do badly.

The Colosseum at opening, when the light is still gold and the queue hasn't formed. Trastevere at dinner, in the middle of the week, at a place that doesn't have a menu in English. Testaccio Market on Saturday morning, with an empty bag and an appetite. That's the trip.

Everything else can wait for the next visit. There will be a next visit.

Chasing the lights, without the cruise.

Tromsø in February: how to read the forecast, where to stand, and why the most expensive aurora tour isn't always the right one.

The aurora doesn't care about your itinerary. The trick to a good lights trip is the same as the trick to a good fishing trip — show up with patience, dress correctly, and be on the water for more nights than you think you'll need. The cabin matters more than the tour.

Five pastries worth a flight.

A canelé in Bordeaux. A pastel de nata in Belém. A kouign-amann in Quimper. The cardamom bun that ruins every other cardamom bun. And the croissant in Paris that isn't where you think.

There are two kinds of pastry travelers. The kind who plans the city around the bakery, and the kind who's lying. I'm the first kind. Most of the best ones aren't on the lists you've seen — they're four blocks off the lists, in the neighborhood the lists tell you to skip on the way to the famous one. This is that map.

Each of these is worth booking a flight around. Not because they're the most photogenic — though some of them are — but because they're the kind of food you can't get anywhere else, made by someone who's been making it the same way for forty years. That's the real ranking.
